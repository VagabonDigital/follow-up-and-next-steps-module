Module Overview

What This Module Builds

A discovery call is not just a list of questions. It is a guided conversation that helps both sides understand the situation more clearly.

In this module

We’ll practise how to start a discovery call, guide the conversation, ask useful questions, deepen vague answers, summarise what the client said, connect value carefully, and confirm a useful next step.

The goal is not to run through a checklist. The goal is to build a reliable way to guide client conversations while staying professional and curious.

Core Framework

The Discovery Call Framework

When a client conversation is open-ended, it is easy to become passive or jump too quickly into pitching. This structure gives the call a clear path.

1
Open
Start warmly and professionally.
“Thanks for taking the time today.”
2
Set Direction
Explain how the conversation will work.
“It would be useful to understand your current situation first, then we can see what makes sense from there.”
3
Discover
Ask about the client’s situation, goals, or challenges.
“Can you tell me what you’re trying to improve right now?”
4
Deepen
Ask follow-up questions when the answer is general.
“When you say the process is slow, what does that usually look like day to day?”
5
Summarise
Reflect back the key point before moving on.
“So if I understand correctly, the main issue is…”
6
Connect
Link value to what the client described.
“Based on what you described, the relevant part is…”
7
Confirm
Create a clear next step.
“Would it make sense to schedule a follow-up where we look at options?”
Framework note
You do not need to use every step perfectly every time. The framework gives you a path to follow when the conversation needs structure.
Remember Open→ Set Direction→ Discover→ Deepen→ Summarise→ Connect→ Confirm
Mini Example — Full Sequence
Client
“We’re comparing a few options, but we’re not sure what we actually need yet.”
Response
“Thanks for sharing that. It would be useful to understand what you’re trying to improve first, then we can see what would make sense. When you say you’re not sure what you need, what is making the decision unclear right now? So if I understand correctly, the main issue is not choosing an option immediately, but getting clearer on your priorities and decision criteria. Based on that, the relevant next step would be to understand what matters most before comparing solutions. Would it make sense to look at those priorities together?”

Situation Map

What You Are Trying to Understand

A discovery call covers several different areas. Knowing which area you are in helps you ask better questions and avoid jumping ahead too quickly.

Tap a card to expand it.

Current Situation
“We’re using a manual process right now.”
You need to learn: what is happening today.
This is usually the starting point. Before you can understand a problem, you need to understand the current state. Ask open questions and listen without jumping to solutions.
Useful move: ask how it currently works
Challenge
“It takes too much time.”
You need to learn: what is not working.
Challenges are often described vaguely at first. Your job is to understand which part of the process creates the difficulty and why it matters to this client specifically.
Useful move: ask where the difficulty appears
Impact
“It slows the team down.”
You need to learn: why the problem matters.
Impact connects the problem to the business. A client who can describe the impact of a problem is more likely to act on it. Help them articulate it by asking what happens when the problem is not resolved.
Useful move: ask what the consequence is
Priority
“We want to improve this soon.”
You need to learn: how important it is.
Not every problem the client describes is a priority. Understanding where this sits relative to other work helps you assess whether a next step is realistic.
Useful move: ask how urgent it is compared with other work
Decision Process
“I need to involve my manager.”
You need to learn: who else is involved.
Knowing the decision process early saves time. If the person you are speaking with cannot move forward alone, it is better to understand that during the discovery call than after it.
Useful move: ask who should be part of the next conversation
Timeline
“Maybe next quarter.”
You need to learn: when action might happen.
Timeline answers are often vague. A useful follow-up is to ask what would need to change for the timing to move forward, rather than accepting the vague answer.
Useful move: ask what would make the timing realistic

Skill 1 of 7

Open the Call Clearly

Key Move: Start the conversation with warmth, purpose, and control.
Why this matters
If the opening is vague, the client may not know what kind of conversation they are in. A clear opening helps the client relax and shows that you can guide the call professionally. Many students rush past the opening because they are eager to ask questions. But a weak opening makes the whole call feel unstructured from the start.
Start warm, then give direction.
Useful language
“Thanks for taking the time today.”
“It’s good to speak with you.”
“I’m looking forward to learning more about your situation.”
“To start, it would be useful to understand where things are now.”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Hi. So, what do you need?”
Stronger
“Thanks for taking the time today. To start, it would be useful to understand your current situation and what you’re hoping to improve.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“Hi, thanks for setting this up.”
Model response
“Thanks for joining. I’d like to understand your situation first, then we can look at what would be useful to discuss.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“Hi, thanks for taking the call.”
Task: Open the call with warmth and a clear sense of purpose. Do not ask a question yet.
“Hi, we only have about 20 minutes today.”
Task: Open the call professionally and set a calm tone even with a time constraint.
“Hi, I have to be honest — I’m not sure why this call was set up.”
Challenge: The client sounds uncertain. Open the call in a way that creates confidence and direction without sounding defensive.
Quick Recap
A strong call starts before the first question.
“To start, it would be useful to understand your current situation.”
Skill 2 of 7

Set Direction

Key Move: Give the conversation a simple path before asking questions.
Why this matters
Without direction, discovery can feel like random questioning. When a student jumps straight into questions without setting direction, the client may feel interrogated rather than guided. A simple structure statement tells the client what is about to happen, why you are asking questions, and where the conversation is heading. This one move changes the tone of the entire call.
Give the client a map.
Useful language
“Maybe we can start with your current situation.”
“Then I’ll ask a few questions to understand the priorities.”
“After that, we can see what would make sense as a next step.”
“Does that sound okay?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“I have some questions for you.”
Stronger
“Maybe we can start with your current situation, then I’ll ask a few questions to understand the priorities. After that, we can see what would make sense as a next step.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“How does this call usually work?”
Model response
“We can keep it simple. I’ll first understand what’s happening on your side, then we can look at whether there’s a useful next step.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The client has just joined the call.
Task: Set a simple direction for the conversation in two or three sentences.
“I’m not sure exactly what you offer.”
Task: Set direction in a way that also gently reassures the client without over-explaining.
“Can we just get to the point?”
Challenge: The client sounds impatient. Set direction quickly and confidently without sounding rushed.
Quick Recap
The client should know where the conversation is going before you ask your first question.
“Maybe we can start with your current situation, then see what would make sense from there.”
Skill 3 of 7

Ask Useful Discovery Questions

Key Move: Ask questions that reveal the client’s situation, problem, priority, and goal.
Why this matters
Generic questions create generic answers. Better questions help the client explain what actually matters, not just what they think they want to buy. A student who only asks “What do you need?” will get surface-level answers. A student who asks about the current situation, what is working, what is not working, and what a better situation would look like will understand the client’s real context.
Understand before you recommend.
Useful language
“Can you tell me what you’re trying to improve?”
“How are you handling this currently?”
“What is working well?”
“What is not working as well as you’d like?”
“What would a better situation look like?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“What do you need?”
Stronger
“Can you tell me what you’re trying to improve and how you’re handling it currently?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“We’re looking for a better system.”
Model response
“That makes sense. Can you tell me what you’re using now and what you’re hoping a better system would improve?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“We’re looking for a better solution.”
Task: Ask two useful discovery questions that help you understand the client’s current situation and what they want to improve.
“We’ve been having some issues internally.”
Task: Ask questions that help the client describe the problem without feeling pressured.
“We’re just exploring options right now.”
Challenge: The client is giving very little away. Ask questions that open the conversation without making them feel committed.
Quick Recap
A better question leads to a better conversation.
“Can you tell me what you’re trying to improve and how you’re handling it currently?”
Skill 4 of 7

Deepen Vague Answers

Key Move: Turn general answers into specific, useful detail.
Why this matters
Clients often begin with broad language: “It’s inefficient,” “we need better visibility,” or “the process is slow.” If you accept that too quickly, you may miss the real issue. Deepening is not interrogating. It is showing genuine curiosity and helping the client articulate something they may not have fully thought through themselves.
Do not stop at the first answer.
Useful language
“When you say that, what does it usually look like day to day?”
“Can you give me an example?”
“How often does that happen?”
“What impact does that have on the team?”
“What happens if this does not improve?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Okay, I understand.”
Stronger
“When you say the process is inefficient, what does that usually look like in practice?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“The process is just too slow.”
Model response
“When you say too slow, what part of the process usually creates the delay?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“It’s just not very efficient.”
Task: Ask one deepening question that turns this vague answer into something specific.
“We need better visibility across the team.”
Task: Ask follow-up questions that help the client explain what visibility means to them and where it breaks down.
“It’s hard to explain. It just doesn’t work well.”
Challenge: The client is struggling to articulate the problem. Ask a gentle deepening question that helps them find the right words without making them feel uncomfortable.
Quick Recap
The real issue is usually one question deeper than the first answer.
“When you say that, what does it usually look like day to day?”
Skill 5 of 7

Summarise What You Heard

Key Move: Reflect the client’s situation back before moving forward.
Why this matters
A summary shows the client they were heard. It also helps you check accuracy before you connect value or suggest a next step. Many students skip the summary because they are eager to move forward. But a good summary builds trust, confirms understanding, and creates a natural transition to the next part of the conversation.
Summarise before you recommend.
Useful language
“So if I understand correctly…”
“The main issue seems to be…”
“What I’m hearing is…”
“It sounds like the priority is…”
“Is that accurate?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Okay, we can help with that.”
Stronger
“So if I understand correctly, your team is losing time because the current process requires too much manual follow-up. Is that accurate?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“We waste a lot of time chasing updates manually.”
Model response
“So the main issue is not only the manual work, but the delays it creates for the team. Is that right?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The client has just explained their situation.
Task: Summarise what you heard in two sentences and check accuracy.
“We have a few issues but the main one is the reporting. It takes too long and the data is never up to date.”
Task: Summarise the client’s situation clearly and confirm before moving forward.
The client has given a long, detailed answer covering several different problems.
Challenge: Summarise the most important point without losing the other details, and check that your summary is accurate.
Quick Recap
A good summary is the bridge between listening and recommending.
“So if I understand correctly… Is that accurate?”
Skill 6 of 7

Connect Value Carefully

Key Move: Link value to what the client actually said.
Why this matters
If you pitch too early, the client may feel sold to instead of understood. A stronger value connection starts from the client’s own problem, not from a generic feature list. The difference between a pitch and a value connection is simple: a pitch starts with your solution, a value connection starts with their problem.
Use their words before your solution.
Useful language
“Based on what you described…”
“The relevant part here is…”
“This matters because you mentioned…”
“Where this could help is…”
“The value would be reducing…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Our platform has many useful features.”
Stronger
“Based on what you described, the relevant part is reducing manual follow-up so your team can get updates faster.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“We spend too much time following up manually.”
Model response
“Based on that, the most relevant value is reducing the manual follow-up so your team can spend less time chasing updates.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“We spend hours every week on manual reporting.”
Task: Connect value to this specific problem without listing features.
“The team is always waiting for updates before they can move forward.”
Task: Connect value in a way that uses the client’s own language and addresses the delay they described.
“I’m not sure your solution is really that different from what we already have.”
Challenge: The client is skeptical. Connect value specifically to the problem they described without sounding defensive.
Quick Recap
Value only lands when it connects to something the client actually said.
“Based on what you described, the relevant part is…”
Skill 7 of 7

Confirm the Next Step

Key Move: End the call with a clear next step.
Why this matters
A discovery call can go well and still end weakly if the next step is unclear. Confirming the next step helps both sides know what will happen after the call. Many students let a good conversation drift to a weak ending because they feel uncomfortable proposing a next step. A clear next step is not pushy — it is professional.
Do not let a good conversation end vaguely.
Useful language
“Would it make sense to schedule a follow-up?”
“The next useful step might be…”
“Should we include anyone else in the next conversation?”
“Would it be helpful if I sent a short summary first?”
“What would be the most useful next step from your side?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Okay, I’ll send you some information.”
Stronger
“I can send a short summary. Would it also make sense to schedule a follow-up so we can review the options together?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“Can you send me something after the call?”
Model response
“Of course. I’ll send a short summary. Would it also be useful to schedule a follow-up so we can go through any questions together?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The discovery call has gone well.
Task: Propose a clear next step without sounding pushy.
“I’ll need to speak with my manager before we go further.”
Task: Confirm the next step in a way that keeps momentum and includes the manager naturally.
“Let me think about it and get back to you.”
Challenge: The client is slowing down. Propose a specific next step that gives them a reason to say yes rather than waiting.
Quick Recap
A good call deserves a clear ending.
“Would it make sense to schedule a follow-up where we look at the options together?”

Full example — Short Answers
Client gives brief responses. See the full sequence in action.
Client
“We need something better.”
Full sequence response
“Thanks for that. Maybe we can start with what you’re comparing at the moment, then see what would make sense from there. When you say you need something better, what is not working with the current options? I want to make sure I understand before suggesting anything. So if I understand correctly, the main issue is that the options look similar, but it is not clear which one fits your priorities best. Based on that, the useful next step would be to define the main criteria before looking at solutions. Would it make sense to do that together?”
Full example — Vague Problem
Client struggles to describe the issue. See how to guide them through.
Client
“We’re just having some internal issues.”
Full sequence response
“I understand. It would be useful to understand the situation a bit better. Can you tell me which area the issues are appearing in — is it more about priorities, communication, timing, or decision-making? When you say internal issues, what does that usually look like in practice? So it sounds like the main challenge is that different people may have different priorities, which makes it harder to agree on the next step. Based on that, the useful thing would be to clarify who needs to be involved and what decision you are trying to make. Would it be helpful to map that out in a follow-up?”

Standard

What Good Looks Like

A strong discovery call should feel guided, curious, and clear. There is no single perfect call — but strong discovery communication usually includes a few clear qualities.

1
Clear opening
“To start, it would be useful to understand your current situation.” — The call starts with warmth and a simple sense of direction.
2
Simple direction
“Maybe we can start with your current situation, then see what makes sense from there.” — The student explains how the conversation will work before asking questions.
3
Useful questions
“Can you tell me what you’re trying to improve and how you’re handling it currently?” — Questions are open and specific, not generic.
4
Deeper follow-up
“When you say the process is slow, what does that usually look like in practice?” — When the client gives a vague answer, the student asks one more question.
5
Accurate summary
“So if I understand correctly… Is that accurate?” — Before moving forward, the student reflects back what they heard and checks accuracy.
6
Value connection
“Based on what you described, the relevant part is…” — Any value mentioned is connected to something the client actually said.
7
Clear next step
“Would it make sense to schedule a follow-up?” — The call ends with a specific, agreed next step rather than a vague ending.
8
Professional tone
The student sounds curious and calm throughout, not passive or pushy.
Strong response checklist

A strong discovery call usually includes at least five of these:

opened with warmth and purpose
gave the conversation a simple direction
asked at least one useful discovery question
followed up when the answer was vague
summarised before recommending
connected value to the client’s situation
confirmed a clear next step
Example Strong Response
Client
“We’re comparing a few options, but we’re not sure what matters most yet.”
Response
“Thanks for sharing that. Before we look at anything specific, it would be useful to understand what your team is trying to improve. When you say you’re not sure what matters most, is the decision more about cost, ease of use, implementation, support, or something else? So if I understand correctly, the main issue is that the options look similar, but the decision criteria are not clear yet. Based on that, a useful next step would be to clarify the priorities before comparing solutions. Would it make sense to do that together?”

Example — Not Sure What We Need
Client
“We’re comparing a few options, but we’re not sure what we actually need yet.”
Response
“Thanks for sharing that. Maybe we can start with what you’re trying to improve, then see what would make sense from there. When you say you’re not sure what you need, what is making the decision unclear right now? So if I understand correctly, the main issue is that the options look similar, but your decision criteria are not fully clear yet. Based on that, the useful next step would be to clarify your priorities before comparing solutions. Would it make sense to look at those together?”
