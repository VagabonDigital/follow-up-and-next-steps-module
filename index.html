Module Overview

What This Module Builds

A good conversation can still lose momentum if the follow-up is vague.

In this module

We’ll practise how to write and say follow-ups that recap the conversation, explain the reason for continuing, confirm the next action, set timing, and keep the conversation moving professionally.

The goal is not to write long emails. The goal is to make the next step clear.

Typical path
3–5 lessons
Depending on the student’s situation and how much practice is needed.
You’ll build
  • Clearer follow-up messages
  • Stronger post-call summaries
  • Better next-step language
  • Less passive “let me know” wording
  • More confident timing phrases
  • Smoother follow-up when someone goes quiet
  • Stronger close-the-loop messages

Core Framework

The Follow-Up Framework

When follow-up is vague, momentum disappears. This structure helps write or say a follow-up that is clear, useful, and easy to respond to.

1
Recap
Summarise the key point from the conversation.
“Thanks again for the conversation today. We discussed…”
2
Reason
Explain why the follow-up matters.
“I’m following up because…”
3
Action
Confirm what should happen next.
“A useful next step would be…”
4
Timing
Set or suggest a clear timeframe.
“Would later this week or early next week work?”
5
Follow Through
Make it easy to continue, pause, reconnect later, or close the loop.
“Happy to adjust if the timing has changed, or reconnect later if that works better.”
Framework note
A good follow-up does not need to be long. It needs to make the next step clear.
Remember Recap→ Reason→ Action→ Timing→ Follow Through
Mini Example — Full Framework
Context
The client asked for a proposal after a demo, but no review meeting was agreed.
Follow-up
“Thanks again for the demo today. We discussed how your team is looking for a smoother way to manage onboarding and support after rollout. I’ve included the proposal below, with the most relevant sections highlighted. A useful next step would be to review any questions together once your team has had a chance to look through it. Would later this week or early next week work for a short follow-up?”

Situation Map

Common Follow-Up Situations

Different situations need different follow-up moves, but the goal is the same: make the next step easier.

Tap a card to see the weak pattern and the better move.

After a Good Call
“Thanks for the call. Let me know what you think.”
Weak pattern: No recap, no action, no reason to reply.
Summarise the key point from the conversation and suggest a specific next step.
Better move: recap + clear next step
After Sending Information
“Please see attached.”
Weak pattern: No direction, no reason, no follow-through.
Explain what they should look at and why. Make it easy to respond without reading everything.
Better move: highlight what matters and why
After a Demo
“Hope the demo was useful.”
Weak pattern: Vague, passive, gives nothing to respond to.
Connect the demo to what they cared about and propose a simple review step.
Better move: connect demo to their priority
After a Proposal
“Just checking if you saw my proposal.”
Weak pattern: Chasing language, no value, no reason to respond.
Reconnect to the decision and offer a simple review step. Give the other person an easy way in.
Better move: reconnect to the decision
After a Meeting With Actions
“Thanks everyone. Good meeting.”
Weak pattern: No clarity on who does what or when.
Summarise the agreed actions, owners, and timing. Even a short version prevents confusion.
Better move: summarise actions, owners, timing
After Internal Approval Needed
“Okay, let me know.”
Weak pattern: No clarity on when to reconnect.
Confirm what they need to do and suggest a clear time to reconnect after they have spoken with their team.
Better move: confirm reconnect timing
After an Interview
“Thanks for the interview. Hope to hear from you.”
Weak pattern: Generic, adds nothing, easy to ignore.
Thank them, briefly reconnect to the role, and ask about the next step in the process.
Better move: reconnect to role + ask next step
After No Reply
“Just following up.”
Weak pattern: No reason, no context, no easy way to respond.
Reconnect to the original reason and give them a simple, low-pressure way to respond or close the loop.
Better move: reconnect + give easy reply option

Skill 1 of 6

Recap the Conversation

Key Move: Summarise the key point from the conversation.
Why this matters
A follow-up should remind the other person what was discussed. If the message starts with no context, they have to do the work of remembering. A clear recap makes the message easier to understand and easier to respond to.
Start with what was discussed.
Useful language
“Thanks again for the conversation today.”
“We discussed…”
“You mentioned that…”
“The main point we covered was…”
“From our conversation, the key issue seems to be…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Thanks for the call.”
Stronger
“Thanks again for the conversation today. We discussed your team’s priorities for the next phase, especially timing and implementation support.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Context
The client explained that implementation timing is important before they decide.
Model follow-up line
“Thanks again for the call today. We discussed your team’s need to understand the implementation timeline before deciding on the next step.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The client said they need to understand the implementation timeline.
Task: Write or say the first two lines of a follow-up message that recap the conversation.
The meeting covered three points: implementation, budget, and timing.
Task: Recap the main point clearly without writing a long paragraph.
The conversation covered several different topics and the client is busy.
Challenge: Recap only the most important point in one sentence.
Quick Recap
A good follow-up starts by reminding the other person what matters.
“From our conversation, the key issue seems to be…”
Skill 2 of 6

Give the Reason for Continuing

Key Move: Explain why you are following up or why the next step matters.
Why this matters
A follow-up that only says “thanks” or “checking in” does not give the other person a reason to respond. A stronger follow-up reconnects to the purpose of the conversation.
Give the follow-up a reason to exist.
Useful language
“I’m following up because…”
“I thought it would be useful to…”
“The reason I’m sending this is…”
“This is relevant because…”
“Based on what you mentioned…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Let me know if you’re interested.”
Stronger
“I thought it would be useful to send the key points we discussed so your team can review them before your internal decision.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Context
The client needs to discuss the proposal internally before deciding.
Model follow-up line
“I’m following up because I thought it would help to organise the main points before your internal discussion.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The client asked for more information.
Task: Write one sentence that explains why you are sending it.
The client needs to speak with their team before deciding.
Task: Give the follow-up a clear reason without sounding pushy.
The client seemed interested but not urgent.
Challenge: Explain the reason for continuing without exaggerating the importance.
Quick Recap
A follow-up should not just remind. It should reconnect to the reason.
“I’m following up because…”
Skill 3 of 6

Confirm the Action

Key Move: Make the next action clear.
Why this matters
Many follow-ups fail because they do not say what should happen next. “Let me know” is easy to ignore because it gives the other person too much work. A clearer action makes the next step easier.
Do not leave the next step vague.
Useful language
“A useful next step would be…”
“The next thing to check would be…”
“Would it make sense to…”
“I suggest we…”
“The next action from my side is…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Let me know what you think.”
Stronger
“A useful next step would be to review the main questions together once you’ve had a chance to look through the information.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Context
The client asked you to send details after a demo.
Model follow-up line
“I’ve included the details below. A useful next step would be to review any questions together after your team has looked through them.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The call ended without a clear next step.
Task: Suggest one specific action.
The client asked you to send information.
Task: Confirm the next action after sending it.
The client is not ready to commit to a full follow-up meeting.
Challenge: Suggest a smaller next action. Examples: review one section, reply with one question, confirm whether timing has changed, choose between a short call and written feedback.
Quick Recap
A clear action is easier to accept than a vague request.
“A useful next step would be…”
Skill 4 of 6

Set Timing Clearly

Key Move: Attach the next action to a clear timeframe.
Why this matters
Without timing, the next step can disappear. Clear timing does not need to be aggressive. It simply helps both sides know when the conversation should continue.
Timing creates momentum.
Useful language
“Would later this week work?”
“Would early next week be realistic?”
“Should we reconnect after you’ve spoken with your team?”
“I can send this today and follow up later this week.”
“Would it be useful to review this together before Friday?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Let’s speak soon.”
Stronger
“Would it be useful to reconnect early next week after you’ve had a chance to review this internally?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Context
The client needs to speak with their manager.
Model follow-up line
“Would it be useful to reconnect next week after you’ve had a chance to speak with your manager?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The client said they need time to review.
Task: Suggest a clear but flexible timeframe.
The client needs to discuss internally before deciding.
Task: Set timing without sounding impatient.
The client says: “I’m not sure when we’ll have time.”
Challenge: Offer a low-pressure timing option.

Model option: “No problem. Would it make sense for me to reconnect in two weeks, or would you prefer to come back to me when the timing is clearer?”
Quick Recap
“Soon” is not a next step.
“Would early next week be realistic?”
Skill 5 of 6

Make It Easy to Answer

Key Move: Write or say the follow-up in a way that makes replying easy.
Why this matters
Even a clear follow-up can fail if the other person does not know how to respond. A good follow-up reduces friction. It gives a simple choice, a clear question, or an easy next action.
Make the reply easy.
Useful language
“Would Tuesday or Thursday work better?”
“Would you prefer a short call or a written summary?”
“Does this still feel relevant?”
“Would it be useful to review this together?”
“Happy to adjust if another time works better.”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Let me know your thoughts.”
Stronger
“Would you prefer to review this together on a short call, or would a written summary be more useful?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Context
You sent a proposal and want to reduce friction for the next step.
Model follow-up line
“Would it be easier to review the proposal together on a short call, or would you prefer to send any questions by email first?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
You want the client to reply.
Task: Turn “Let me know your thoughts” into an easier question.
You sent a proposal after a call.
Task: Give the client two simple response options.
The client is busy and may not want another meeting.
Challenge: Offer a low-friction way to continue.

Model option: “If a call is not useful right now, feel free to send me the main question by email and I’ll respond directly.”
Quick Recap
The easier the reply, the more likely the conversation continues.
“Would you prefer… or…?”
Skill 6 of 6

Handle Silence or Delay

Key Move: Restart, pause, or close the loop when the other person has gone quiet or delayed the next step.
Why this matters
Silence does not always mean rejection. It may mean the person is busy, the timing changed, or the priority dropped. A strong message gives them an easy way to re-engage, delay clearly, or close the loop professionally.
Give them an easy way back in.
Useful language
“Is this still something you’d like to explore?”
“Has the timing changed on your side?”
“Should I reconnect later?”
“Would it be better to revisit this next month?”
“Happy to close the loop for now if priorities have changed.”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Any update?”
Stronger
“Has the timing changed on your side, or would it still be useful to reconnect on this next week?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Context
The client has gone quiet after receiving a proposal.
Model follow-up line
“Has the timing changed on your side, or would it still be useful to review the proposal together next week?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
The client has gone quiet after a good call.
Task: Write one line that gives them an easy way to respond.
The client delayed the decision because of internal priorities.
Task: Restart the conversation without pressure.
The client may no longer be interested.
Challenge: Send a graceful close-the-loop message.

Model option: “I understand priorities may have changed. I’m happy to close the loop for now, and we can revisit this later if it becomes relevant again.”
Quick Recap
Silence needs clarity, not panic.
“Has the timing changed on your side?”

Full Example — Internal Meeting Follow-Up
See the framework used for a team meeting with action items.
Context
A team meeting created several action items, but ownership and timing need to be clear.
Follow-up
“Thanks everyone for the discussion today. To keep the next steps clear, I’ve summarised the agreed actions, owners, and target dates below. The main priority is to confirm the first draft by Friday so we can review it early next week. Please let me know if I’ve missed anything or if any timing needs to change.”
Spoken Version — End of a Call
Using the framework out loud before finishing the conversation.
At the end of a call
“Before we finish, a useful next step might be for me to send the most relevant information, then we can schedule a short follow-up to review any questions. Would later this week or early next week work?”

Practice Upgrade

Follow-Up Challenge Bank

Once the response structure is clear, try the same prompt with one extra difficulty. Add one challenge at a time.

Tap any card to expand it.

Short Message
The follow-up must be under 80 words.

Keep the recap, next action, and timing. Remove anything that does not help the person respond.

Busy Client
The person may not read a long message.

Make the first sentence useful. Give them one clear reason to keep reading.

Skeptical Client
The person is not convinced the next step is worth it.

Reconnect the next step to their original priority. Do not push. Make the reason useful.

Internal Approval
The person needs to speak with someone else first.

Support their process. Suggest a clear reconnect time after their internal conversation.

No Reply
The person has already ignored one message.

Reconnect to the original reason and give them an easy way to continue, delay, or close the loop.

Too Many Details
The previous conversation had too many points and needs a clean summary.

Choose the one or two points that affect the next step. Do not summarise everything.

Soft Close
The other person is not ready, but you want to keep the door open.

Acknowledge the timing and leave a clear, professional way to reconnect later.

No Meeting
The other person does not want another call.

Offer a lower-friction option, such as written feedback, one question by email, or a simple confirmation.

Senior Tone
The follow-up should sound calm, concise, and executive.

Use fewer words, remove soft filler, and make the action and timing clear.

Interview Tone
The message should sound professional and warm, but not desperate.

Thank them, reconnect briefly to the role or conversation, and ask about the next step without overexplaining.

What Good Follow-Up Looks Like

A strong follow-up should make the next step easier, not heavier.

1
Clear recap
The message reminds the other person what was discussed.
2
Clear reason
The message explains why the follow-up matters.
3
Specific action
The next step is clear and easy to understand.
4
Clear timing
The message avoids vague timing like “soon” or “when you have a chance.”
5
Easy response
The other person knows how to reply without extra effort.
6
Professional tone
The follow-up sounds confident, not needy or pushy.
7
Follow-through
The message gives the conversation a clear way to continue, pause, or close.
Strong follow-up checklist
recaps the conversation
gives a clear reason for continuing
confirms the next action
sets or suggests timing
makes it easy to respond
keeps the tone professional
avoids chasing language
allows the other person to continue, delay, or close the loop
